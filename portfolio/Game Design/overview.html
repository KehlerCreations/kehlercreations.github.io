Level design for Two Dollar Suits

The level design for Two Dollar Suits requires intricate and careful planning. Players interact with a large amount of mechanics, and every map can be completed in multiple ways.

I designed a large casino heist and many others using this sketch format. On this image, you can see NPC placement, security items, AI interactions and more.

Here I showcase one of many levels in the form of a one minute speedrun. The level requires interaction with many different mechanics, which are positioned deliberately throughout the map to encourage exploration, and are balanced for both stealthy and violent tactics.

Level design for a large bank heist. This mission was designed with symmetry in mind. Players have equal access to world interaction on both the left and right sides.

I sometimes also draw my map concepts on paper. This example comes from a special mission designed for a usability study on the mechanics of A-Star Theft. You can find the full study in Swedish here.

To help players plan their actions on larger maps, I have developed an "Overview" system, where players can navigate around the map, find important objects and set custom markers.

Level design is both about good interaction layout and theme-appropriate decoration.

Weapons system for Two Dollar Suits

Two Dollar Suits features a complex weapons and equipment system designed around the idea of credits. Players have a certain amount of credits available, and different tools require different amounts to use. The game encourages experimentation, as credits are returned once an item is unequipped.

I have also developed a skill system, where my focus has been on making creative and unique new tools available for players. I avoid such skills that only add marginal increases in performance, but instead enable massive changes in gameplay, such as in the skill displayed above, where missed shots become a significant threat to players.

With the amount of choice present in Two Dollar Suits, proper economy balancing has been a large focus area. Players must get proportionate rewards relative to their skill, time required for completion, and how far into the game they are.

Narrative writing for Two Dollar Suits

The narrative is central to the experience in Two Dollar Suits. I have built up the city of San Dinero over many years.

I organize this writing in Trello, using columns and tags to keep track of the world and events.

Dialog and character actions are integrated using JSON. With this system, I can select facial expressions, set mission objectives, and tell characters to look at each other.

Sense abilities for Walking With The Wild

One of the unique features of Walking With The Wild is its sense ability system. The player is gifted with enhanced senses, which must be used to navigate and survive the journey. The game is designed to use our real senses as much as is reasonably possible. Vision is obviously part of this, but so is listening carefully to sound effects and feeling controller vibrations to detect dangers from afar.

The system includes Wwise integration for the Audition sense, to amplify creature audio and attenuate background noise.

World map design for Walking With The Wild

Designing the world for Walking With The Wild is an ambitious endeavor, but one that I find incredibly rewarding. For this, I have to create interesting environments that can naturally flow from biome to biome.

I use extensive references and organize these using Obsidian.

Size references are used to keep a consistent and realistic scale.

Much of the world map is designed physically, sculpted in clay and then 3D scanned. This gives the game an authentic and grounded touch.
